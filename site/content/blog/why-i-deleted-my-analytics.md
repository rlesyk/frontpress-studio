For about three years I had a small, privacy-respecting analytics package on this site. It was unobtrusive. It loaded fast. It did not set cookies. It told me which posts people were reading and roughly where in the world they were. I liked it the way one likes a comfortable habit — without thinking about why.

Last month I deleted it.

## Why

Two reasons, in roughly increasing weight.

The first is performance. The script was small and asynchronous, but it was still a thing the browser had to fetch and execute on every page load. The site is supposed to be fast. Removing the script took 14 kilobytes off the wire and a non-zero amount of CPU off the cold-start of every page. That is not a lot. It is also not nothing.

The second is the more important reason. I noticed that the analytics dashboard was changing what I wrote. I would publish an essay, refresh the dashboard, and quietly note which posts had pulled in readers and which had not. Over time the high-performing posts had a kind of gravitational pull. I would catch myself drafting something in their shape, even when the original idea wanted to be something different.

I did not write down topics that I knew would not perform. That is the part I am embarrassed about. The audience metrics had become a soft censor that I had not even noticed installing.

## What I miss

Almost nothing. Once a month I would have liked to know how many people read a particular post. The site has an Atom feed and an inbound link or two; that is enough signal to know whether I am writing into a void.

A few times in the first week I caught myself reaching for the dashboard out of habit. Each time the absence of it was a small relief — like the moment after a notification stops chiming.

## What I do not miss

Watching numbers, mostly. The dashboard had become a low-grade slot machine. Removing it took back a small amount of attention, which is the only resource I actually have to write with.

If you publish things on the open web and you have noticed yourself writing for the dashboard, consider the experiment.
