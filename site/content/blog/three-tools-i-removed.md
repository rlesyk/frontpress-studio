Every six months or so I do a sweep of the apps and services I am paying attention to. Anything I have not opened in a month gets a hard look. The criterion is simple: am I using this, or have I just gotten used to it being there? This round, three things lost.

## A clipboard manager

I had a clipboard history app that kept the last hundred things I copied. In theory this was supposed to save me hits to the search box. In practice I spent more time scrolling through the history looking for the snippet I half-remembered copying than I would have spent copying it again. The hit rate was low, the cognitive overhead was constant, and there is something uniquely wrong about an app that watches everything you copy.

What replaced it: nothing. I copy something, I paste it, the buffer turns over. If I need it twice, I write it down somewhere durable.

## A second todo app

I had two task systems running in parallel — one for work, one for personal — and they never agreed on what was urgent. Inbox triage was happening in two places. Things would slip through the cracks because I had checked one and not the other. Worse, the existence of two apps gave me a way to procrastinate by re-organising both instead of doing the work.

What replaced it: one app, one inbox, tagged. The conceptual cost of a tag is much lower than the cost of an entire second context.

## A status-page subscription service

I was paying a small monthly fee to be alerted when third-party services I depend on had outages. The alerts almost never arrived before our own internal monitoring picked up the resulting failures, and when they did arrive first, the gap was rarely meaningful. We were going to find out either way.

What replaced it: nothing again. If the third party is down, I will know within ninety seconds because something in our own logs will be on fire. The push notification was solving the wrong problem.

## What survived

The things that made it through were the ones I would notice missing within an hour: a terminal, a browser, an editor, a password manager, a calendar. Everything else has to keep justifying itself.
