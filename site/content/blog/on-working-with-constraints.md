The first draft of anything I write is too long. The first version of every program I write is too clever. The first plan I make for a project always tries to do everything. Left to my own devices, I am a maximalist by default, and almost everything I am proud of has come from someone or something forcing me to be less.

This was true when a publisher told me an essay had to fit in twelve hundred words. It was true when a deployment target gave me eight megabytes of memory. It was true when a budget meeting cut a project from six people to two. Each time the first reaction was frustration. Each time the actual outcome was better than what I had been planning.

## What constraints actually do

Constraints do two useful things. The first is obvious: they cut the work. You stop doing the parts that do not fit. The second is less obvious but more important: they reveal which parts you actually believed in. When you have to remove half of what you planned, the half you keep tells you what the project really is.

Most projects I have worked on were too vague at the start. We had ideas about what they could do, but no clear sense of what they had to do. Constraints make that sense for you. They do not always pick the same thing you would have, but the choice is now visible, and you can argue about it.

## Writing your own

The trick I have learned slowly is that you can give yourself constraints when nobody else is. I now start most projects by writing down a single sentence of what the project is, and one sentence of what it is not. The "is not" sentence does more work. It tells me which features I am allowed to have ideas about and which ones I am supposed to leave alone.

For example: a personal site is "a place to write essays" and not "a portfolio, a wiki, a comment system, or a newsletter platform." The "is not" list keeps me from re-architecting the site every quarter to absorb a new ambition.

## When to relax them

Self-imposed constraints become a problem when they outlive their usefulness. Mine usually do, because I forget to revisit them. Once a year I look at the "is not" list and ask whether any of the items have started to feel arbitrary. If they have, I remove them. The constraint was useful while it was working; once it stops working, keeping it just for the discipline is the kind of false-economy thinking that produces the maximalism it was trying to prevent.

The goal is not to be small forever. It is to make the size of the work match the size of what you can hold in your head at once. Constraints are how you measure that.
